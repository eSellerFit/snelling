03 — Market Pressure Model
Five pressures applied to Snelling Westchester + CT
1. Recruiting / advertising cost
High · 7.5
Sourcing workers in the NYC metro is expensive. Indeed Sponsored Jobs, ZipRecruiter, Facebook geo-ads, background checks ($25/hire), drug screens ($35/hire) add $100–200 per placed worker. Fixed platform costs of $800+/month run regardless of fill rate. Your single largest operational cost pressure. Mitigated by referral bonuses — once you have 5+ active workers, worker-to-worker referrals are your cheapest and most reliable channel.
2. Worker supply — transportation barrier
Medium-High · 6.5
Your worker pool (Yonkers, Mount Vernon, South Bronx) earns median $45–60K — they need your job but many lack cars. 6am warehouse shifts are when Metro-North runs infrequently. White Plains HQ solves part of this — workers can reach your office by train or bus, then you dispatch. High no-show rates are the #1 cause of early failure for light industrial agencies. Build a 30% bench surplus over active placements from Day 1.
3. Client demand quantity
Favorable · 2.5
Demand is strong and structural. Industrial vacancy near record lows means new employers keep arriving. 34,000 Westchester firms, multiple 3PLs, manufacturers, food distributors all within 15 miles. The question in this market is never whether clients exist — it is whether you can fill orders fast enough. Client demand pressure is low. Supply-side execution is your real constraint.
4. Margin corridor — HQ fee stack
Medium · 5.5
HQ takes 4.5% of payroll (funding cost) + 18% of gross margin (royalty) + up to 3% health pool + 2% WC/security = roughly 33–37% of gross margin before your fixed costs. At $20 pay / $28 bill ($8 GM/hr) you keep ~$4.51/hr Franchisee's Share. Workable at scale — requires 1,700+ billable hours/month to cover typical fixed costs. Break-even at ~8–10 employees at full hours.
⚖️
5. Competitive intensity — nationals + strong regionals already entrenched
Adecco, Randstad, Manpower, Express Employment, Merritt Staffing (CT), and Hamilton Connections (CT) are all active in your market — some with 25+ years of client relationships. You win not on price or brand, but on the employer-of-record differentiator (no client WC or payroll exposure) and owner-operator responsiveness (you answer at 6am when a client's shift is short; Adecco sends them to a call center). Pressure: Medium-High (6.5). Winnable through account-by-account relationship building, not mass marketing.

08 — Strategic Insights
Key factors shaping your launch
Employer-of-record is your only un-copyable advantage
Every competitor shifts some WC and payroll burden back to the client. HireQuest's model means the client has zero payroll exposure, zero WC claims risk, and zero HR admin for temp workers. This is legally and operationally different — not just a talking point. Open every conversation with it. Clients who've had a workers' comp claim from a temp will sign immediately.
$17/hr floor changes the break-even calculation
Westchester's minimum wage means realistic pay rates of $19–22/hr for reliable workers. At $20 pay / $28 bill your gross margin is $8/hr — tighter than most US markets. You make money on volume and fill rate, not margin per hour. Getting to 20+ active workers fast is the strategic imperative. Every worker below break-even costs you money on fixed costs.
White Plains solves the transportation problem
Light industrial staffing's #1 operational failure mode is worker no-shows — especially on early morning warehouse shifts when transit is limited. White Plains is served by Metro-North directly from Yonkers (7 min) and has Bee-Line bus connections from Mount Vernon. Workers can reach your office to check in before dispatch. This reduces your no-show rate vs an office in suburban Scarsdale or Armonk.
3PL clients are your fastest path to recurring revenue
3PL warehouses (Keurig Dr Pepper, Unis Logistics, Ultrafabrics) have structurally variable demand — their headcount swings with client volumes. They sign faster (1–3 weeks), have recurring needs, and can scale from 2 to 10 workers within a single account. Anchor your book here before investing time in corporate HQ accounts with 3–6 month procurement cycles.
HireQuest's shrinking system is a franchisor risk to monitor
The FDD shows the Snelling system declined from 78 → 73 franchises 2023–2025, and HireQuest's royalty revenue fell 19% ($35.8M → $29.0M). This doesn't make Snelling a bad business, but it means the franchisor's support infrastructure is under financial pressure. HireQuest is publicly traded (NASDAQ: HQI) — review their quarterly filings quarterly as a franchisee.
Referral bonuses are your cheapest recruiting channel — activate early
Once you have 5+ active workers, launch a structured $50–75 referral bonus paid after 30 days on assignment. Worker-to-worker referrals in light industrial are the most reliable and lowest-cost sourcing channel — far cheaper than Indeed or Facebook at $100–200 per placed worker. Turn your workers into your best recruiters from Month 1.
09 — Risk Register
What could go wrong
R1
Worker no-show rate destroys client relationships
A no-show at a 6am warehouse shift damages a client relationship immediately and permanently. Build a bench roster 30% larger than active placements. Over-recruit, under-promise on capacity. Fill rate — not bill rate — determines whether you keep an account. White Plains HQ helps here: workers can check in at your office before dispatch, reducing ghost no-shows.
R2
WC loss ratio above 65% triggers punishing pass-through charges
If your workers' WC claims exceed 65% of premium, HQ charges you 100% of excess, capped at 130% of annual premium (or 200% if your premium was under $30K). Warehouse and light industrial carry real injury risk — forklift incidents, repetitive strain, slips. Vet placement sites carefully, avoid restricted WC codes, and actively manage light-duty return-to-work. The $75/day fee for not providing light duty is explicitly in the FDD.
R3
5-week cash lag creates a launch crisis
HireQuest pays your Franchisee's Share ~5 weeks after billing. You pay your staff, rent, and recruiting ads monthly from Day 1. At launch you will fund 5–6 weeks of fixed costs before your first check. At $8,700+/month in fixed costs, that's $10–15K you need in reserve beyond the FDD's minimum working capital estimate. Undercapitalize this and you have a cash crisis before your first placement delivers.
R4
Competing nationals have 25-year client relationships
Adecco, Manpower, and Merritt Staffing have been in this market for decades. Some clients have procurement agreements that make switching painful even if you're better. Don't waste time on locked accounts — identify which clients are actively frustrated with their current agency (ask on every canvass visit) and go deep there first. One dissatisfied Adecco client is worth more than ten cold prospects.
R5
HireQuest's declining system revenue may reduce support quality
With 5 net franchise closures in 2025 and royalty revenue down 19% over two years, HQ's support infrastructure is under financial pressure. Before signing, confirm: how many support staff are dedicated to franchisees? What is the current ratio? A franchisor with declining revenue may quietly reduce support responsiveness over your first year.
R6
CT operations without employment agency license
Connecticut requires an Employment Agency License to operate temp staffing in the state. Placing workers in CT before securing this license exposes you and potentially HQ to regulatory action. Confirm the licensing requirement and whether the Snelling system covers it — before making your first CT placement, not after.
R7
Open-ended technology fee
The Technology Fee is disclosed as "allocation of actual cost" — HQ can change it with notice, and it's non-refundable. If HQ undertakes major technology investments, your monthly tech cost could increase significantly. Model it conservatively at $300–500/month and watch for any changes in quarterly HireQuest earnings calls.
